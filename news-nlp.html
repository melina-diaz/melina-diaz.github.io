The problem

Why does news language matter?

Every news category has its own vocabulary fingerprint. But which words do publishers actually lean on to capture attention and do those words differ across categories?

This project quantifies those patterns by mapping 100,000 news headlines into a shared language space, revealing how Science, Business, Entertainment, Health, Sports, World Politics, and National Politics establish their own distinct corner.

Potential applications
Automatic content tagging for publishers
Brand media monitoring by topic
Audience language profiling
Editorial bias detection

The approach

From raw headlines to a word space

01
Text cleaning & preprocessing

Removed stopwords, URLs, punctuation, and numeric tokens from raw headlines using NLTK and regex. Lowercased and normalised encoding artifacts.

02
Word frequency extraction per category

Combined all headlines per topic and counted word frequencies using CountVectorizer. Words appearing fewer than 5 times were filtered to reduce noise.

03
TF-IDF normalisation

Applied TF-IDF weighting to surface words that are distinctive to each category rather than just common across all news.

04
Dimensionality reduction & visualisation

Compressed the high-dimensional word frequency vectors using PCA to 3 components, then plotted each word as an interactive point coloured and sized by category using Plotly.

Key libraries

Key finding

What the clusters reveal

The visualisation surfaces something intuitive but rarely quantified: news categories don't just differ in topic, they differ in language culture.

Health and Science cluster closely in vocabulary space, while Sports and Business sit at opposite ends — suggesting that news language reflects not just topic, but underlying audience, tone, and rhetorical convention.

These patterns could power an automatic content tagging system, a brand media monitoring tool, or a newsroom editorial assistant — anywhere that understanding the texture of language at scale provides an edge.
